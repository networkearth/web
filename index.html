My Research

To manage fisheries effectively, we must understand them deeply. My research is grounded in the belief that such understanding begins with directly observing and interpreting individual behavior. One must listen to understand. Yet in the marine environment, this is an incredibly difficult task — the ocean is a massive, harsh, dynamic place. Therefore, my goal is to develop tools and techniques that make detailed observation and interpretation of individual behavior possible, and to leverage that understanding into more effective, species-specific management.

Active Projects

Depth Occupancy of Chinook Salmon in the Gulf of Alaska

Chinook salmon bycatch in Alaska's pollock fishery poses serious ecological and regulatory challenges. Because Chinook and pollock occupy different parts of the water column, adding depth data to existing bycatch mitigation strategies could offer a powerful new tool. This project aims to build a predictive model of Chinook depth occupancy using environmental and temporal context—enabling localized, dynamic assessments of bycatch risk.

Open Source Tags

Current biologging tags are expensive and proprietary, limiting who can collect and learn from animal movement data. We're developing open-source, low-cost tags that log depth, temperature, and light—designed for marine environments and small enough to attach to fish. By making data access and hardware design transparent, we aim to empower researchers, fishers, and communities to generate their own insights into animal behavior and ecosystem dynamics.

Movement of Chinook Salmon in the Gulf of Alaska

To manage species well, we need to understand how they move. We're building probabilistic movement models that estimate where animals are likely to go, based on environmental context and observed behavior. These models go beyond simple tracks—they can help identify high-risk zones, uncover behavioral patterns, and potentially inform real-time management decisions.

Past Projects

Mimic: Modeling Individual Behavior

A lightweight framework for building discrete choice, probabilistic models of animal behavior originally developed to analyze fish movement from satellite tag data. By structuring behavioral decisions as choices among alternatives with feature vectors, mimic makes it easy to train models, run inference, and explore behavior across space and time. Ideal for rapid iteration and exploratory analysis, it generalizes across species and behavioral contexts.
